Project Focus

Target Audience

New and returning RIT Students

Stakeholders

  • RIT Management
    • Faculty
    • Administration
  • RIT Students
    • New students
      • International
      • Local residents
    • Returning students

What the stakeholders want:

  • Students want accurate information to be accessible in a single space
  • Students want information in a structured manner
  • Students want to keep a progress check on information receieved
  • RIT administration wants to provide information required by the students
  • RIT administration wants to provide structured information to students in a timely manner

What problems they face:

  • Students are overwhelmed with the various information channels that exist
  • Students cannot keep track of the required steps to be carried before and after arriving on campus
  • RIT administration worry that information is not dispersed effectively to the students in a timely manner

What they propose:

  • A universally accessible portal for information for all RIT students
  • Receiving information in a timely manner through push notification, text messages, and emails

How to involve the stakeholders:

  • Building Prototypes and sharing with stakeholders to get feedback on improvements

How to communicate progress:

  • Updating the project website to act as a medium for communicating the project progress

How to understand their way of communication:

  • Performing interviews to get qualitative data
  • Performing surveys to get quantitative data
  • Organizing focus groups to get deep insight to understand important issues they face

How to communicate the design to them:

  • Conducting a usability test and involving the stakeholders in the design phase

Problem Statement

  • The resources required by the new and returning students of RIT are made available to them by the different departments through their individual websites.
  • The information, even though available, is not well structured and the students must know what to search for to find that piece of information.
  • The students recieve a lot of information from different sources, which becomes overwhelmingly large in number and it becomes difficult to keep track of them, which eventually leads to them missing important communications.

Direction for a solution

  • Identify and gather information required by the students
  • Present information in a structured manner
    • Present the information in a card-based interface
    • Categorize information into relevant groups
    • Keep a check on the progress of checklist items
  • Provide notification through push notification, text messages, and emails for effective communication

Conducting Interviews

Our team members conducted interviews on various stakeholders. We asked a series of questions to the interviewees and notes were taken and important points were highlighted. Each interview session lasted for about 10 - 15 minutes.

Interview Questions

How were you informed about the student computer account? What issues did you face while creating the computer account? How did you know about setting the RIT computer account? How did you get information for registering for orientation? Information about submitting transcripts and degree certificates, how accessible were these? Did you get enough information regarding health insurance? Did you know about the early arrival accommodation for on-campus students? Did you sign up for the free student pick up from the airport? How effective was the mode of communication with the administration of RIT? Did you get enough information about the steps required to complete the collection of the RIT ID card? Were you aware about document submission at the ISS prior to arriving on campus? How did you learn about my-courses and how accessible was it? Did you find any difficulty updating your details on the SIS for course enrollment? Did you get enough information about tuition fees payment on e-services? What all resources are used to disperse information to all the students? What major problems occur while contacting international students? What are the major areas where students face problems? What is the main communication channel between the departments and the administration? What are the problems that students have when they reach out to you? What are the problems faced by the students during the application process? Whom do the students usually contact if they have a problem before coming to college? In what way is the application process different for undergraduate students from the graduate students? How do you keep track of students admission process? How effective is the mode of communication if the student does not have an RIT email? What happens if the student defers the admission? Are automatic emails setup for deferred students?

Analyzing Data

After conducting the interviews, our team then spent time transcribing these into scripts to allow us to analyze the data effectively. We then went ahead to break these scripts into sentences and then broke these sentences down further to allow us to get reasonable data about issues these participants were facing and comment about the existing systems that are currently in place.

Affinity Notes

We then proceeded on writing these analyzed data on the post-it notes (Affinity notes) and then put everything on the board as part of the first initial grouping (which was a more general grouping).

Bottom Level Labels (Blue labels)

We proceeded on putting general ideas together and putting these general ideas under a common category by identifying points that were common with some of our interview participants. This gave us a basic understanding of the demographic data. We used the blue post-it notes to identify this group.

Temporary Top Level Labels (Green labels)

For the temporary top level, we had grouped the bottom level into a more broader and common idea category and created temporary category grouping which we had put in the temporary green post-it note. This allowed us to identify relationships between the bottom level groups.

Middle Level Labels (Pink labels)

The Middle level was about bringing the temporary top-level grouping into a more organized and descriptive grouping that allowed us to get a better understanding of the user's needs.

Top Level Labels (Green labels)

For the final step of the interpretation, we used the green post-it note to combine the ideas gotten from the grouping done on the middle level above. This gave a more specific description of what the users wanted and the general solution we should focus on.

Process for creating the personas:

First, we summarized the interviews and named all the interview subjects as U1, U2, U3, U4 & U5.

Roles:

  • People using social media to get information.( U1,U2,U5)

  • People preferring Mobile over PCs/ Laptop.(U2)

We identified the different behavioral variables from our interview summaries.

We then used points on a scale for each behaviour variable to map the subjects to their behaviour.

Lo-Fi Prototype

TigerSteps provides step by step instructions needed by international students coming to RIT, to complete the admission process and settle-in easily. This app keeps track of the students’ progress through the admission process and ensures that the necessary information about settling into RIT is properly dissipated to the student. It also helps to minimize the problem of students missing crucial steps required by the administration while going through the admission process.

For us to understand the performance of the system design, a usability test was conducted on the prototype. This was done to understand how easy the system was and also to get real-world constraints that may exist for all the stakeholders using TigerSteps.

Planning the prototype:

In the group’s first meeting, we consolidated all the pain points from our personas and came up with the basic hierarchy of the interaction flow.

We identified all the app screens that would be relevant to the problem statement and created a rough sketch for each.

We further reiterated the designs and identified the screens we wanted to represent on our low-fidelity prototype.

Planning the prototype:

  • TigerSteps is designed to be a mobile application for iOS and Android; this was conceived to enable portability and meet with the convenience of accessing the information at any point in time.
  • For this, the prototype consisted of a series of paper cutouts that represented different mobile screens and proposed interactions.
  • The screens were scaled up so that the users would get a good understanding of all the elements and interactions available on the app.
  • A decision was made for all the interactive elements (like buttons and links) to be represented in Pink.

Usability testing:

  • For the usability test, three participants were recruited to interact with the Lo-Fi prototype.
  • Two participants were from the IST department and hence had very good knowledge of developing mobile applications and one participant was from electrical department.
  • All the interviews were conducted in the Library, with a large table that enabled proper interaction with the prototype interface.
  • All the team members were present for the interview and one of them was collecting data from the interview.
  • All the participants were given a brief introduction about the application before interacting with the prototype.
  • After answering the questions that the participants had, they were presented with the prototype and were asked to interact with it.
  • Various nonverbal cues were studied on how they reacted and how long it took to complete a particular task.
  • Once they had gone through the interface, the participants were asked about the ease of completing a task and the clarity of UI.

Results of usability testing:

Through the user interviews certains gaps were identified in the flow of user interaction.

  • Initially the progress bar was made static, but one of the participants mentioned that they would like to see all the pending tasks at one place, which prompted a change to the initial design to provide an expanded list of all the remaining tasks.
  • We were also able to identify the best design for the task details page from the two provided options. Two out of the three participants preferred the full page design as opposed to the pop-up design.
  • All the users faced difficulty in mapping the login page of RIT computer account to the app’s home page, hence the proper connection was added to show the change of screen.

Final lo-fi prototype:

Breakdown of individual screens:

  • The RIT computer account credentials is used as a mode of authentication on the app.
  • For new incoming students, who have not activated their computer account yet, a link to the webpage is provided. The steps required to be able to activate the RIT computer account are listed on the app screen which informs the user, required steps needed to activate the account.
  • Once the RIT computer account is active, the user is taken to the login portal. User’s login credentials will be saved, so that they do not have to login every time they open the app.
  • After logging in, the user arrives at the homepage of the app. They are provided with two options, one for viewing the tasks required to be done before arriving on campus, and another option, similarly for post-arrival. At the bottom of the page there is a progress bar which shows the total number of tasks the user has completed. The hamburger menu presents additional options which might be useful for the user. An option to access the external links to the various services that are available from other RIT resources.
  • The pre-arrival and post-arrival screens present the tasks in a card based format. The cards are ordered according to their relevance, and once a card is marked as done, it moves to the end of the list. Each card contains a short description about the task along with an indication if the task is completed or pending. When the user clicks on a card, they are presented with the full details about the task and an option to mark it as done. If the user wants to undo a task, they can view the task again, and this time they’ll be given the option to undo.
  • We conducted an A/B testing on the task details screen, first presents a separate screen opens up and the other with a pop-up screen on top of the card-view.
  • The users can also search for a particular task from the search bar provided on top of the screen. The user has the ability to access the list of all available tasks from the dropdown menu organised in alphabetical order. Once the user starts typing on the search bar, all the matching tasks appear on the dropdown, and if the user clicks on any one option, it opens up the task details page for that task.
  • The progress bar shows the number of tasks completed in each section for pre arrival and post arrival respectively. Clicking on the bar will pull up a list remaining and completed tasks, belonging to that section. The user can switch between the tabs to see the list of completed tasks.
  • A floating chat button was added on each screen, so that the students can access the chat with their PALs (Peer Advisory Leaders) at any time, and ask them any questions that they have regarding any tasks.
